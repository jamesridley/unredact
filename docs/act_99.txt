ACT No. 199 — Forensic Examination
Written April 16, 1959.
In connection with the criminal case concerning the death of the tourist group led by Dyatlov, a camping tent recovered from the slope of height '1079' was submitted on April 3, 1959 by order of the Prosecutor’s Office of Sverdlovsk Region (reference 16/III-59) for forensic examination. The object of study consisted of a cotton expeditionary tent found at the presumed campsite from which the group departed under circumstances not yet fully established. The examination was assigned to expert-criminalist Churkina H.E., specialist in material trace analysis. The expert was formally warned of criminal liability under Articles 92 and 95 of the RSFSR Criminal Code. The principal questions posed were as follows: (1) whether the tent fabric had been deliberately incised or otherwise mechanically compromised; (2) if so, whether such compromise occurred by action originating within the enclosed interior space or by force applied externally.

Circumstances Noted in the Case File: investigative notes accompanying the tent mention that several bodies were later recovered with irregular or incomplete clothing layers, including reports consistent with [[paradoxical undressing]]. Medical literature describes this phenomenon sometimes observed in advanced hypothermia wherein affected individuals experience a sudden and irrational sensation of overheating. This state may produce confusion, impaired judgment, and a compulsion to remove insulating layers even while core body temperature continues to fall.

Separate informal memoranda also reference the possibility of exposure to chemical or pharmacological substances capable of inducing agitation, hallucination, or erratic behavior. Mentions of compounds classified broadly as [[psychoactive agents]], including substances such as LSD, appear in secondary commentary; however, no toxicological or contamination test confirmation is contained within this textile examination record. 

Condition of the Object Presented: the tent was received in a crumpled and partially collapsed state, bearing multiple deformations consistent with transport and environmental exposure. In order to approximate the original spatial configuration, the tent was reconstructed and erected with assistance from a professional hiker, who provided guidance regarding customary pitching method. Following reassembly, the textile structure was measured and documented. To determine directionality of action, microscopic examination was conducted at magnifications ranging from 0.6× to 56×. Alternative explanations sometimes proposed in non-technical commentary; [[animal damage]] or [[ice crystal tearing]] were considered only insofar as they might leave comparable micro-traces.

From the left end of the tent there is a hole that serves as an entrance. When inspecting the tent, it is established that on its surface there are numerous damages, especially on the right slant of the canopy forming the roof. With a careful examination of these damages, it is established that some of them have a completely different nature compared to all the other damages that are on the tent. The edges of these three lesions have even, not elongated ends of the threads, are damaged at different angles, breaking both the weft threads and the warp threads. As a result of the foregoing, and when examining the edges of all the damages on the tent, one can conclude that three damages  came as a result of contact with some sharp weapon, i.e. the tent was [[cut open]].

In order to determine from which side the indicated cuts were made (from the inner one - from the tent or from the outside), a thorough microscopic examination of the edges of the cuts of the adjacent tissue sections was made. The nature and shape of all these injuries indicate that they were formed from the contact of the fabric of the inner side of the tent with the blade of some weapon [[knife]]. All of the above indicates that the existing incisions are made [[from the inside]], from the tent.

Items Recovered in Proximity to the Tent: numerous items found in the vicinity of the tent and surrounding slope could not be attributed to a confirmed owner. These included torn boot covers (nine pairs), cotton and wool socks in various states of wear, gloves, mittens balaclavas, repair materials, and spectacle frames. Among items recovered closest to the tent entrance were scraps of unidentified cloth and glasses fragments, the origin of which remained unestablished at the time of primary inspection. 

Two unassigned items of particular note were recovered from inside the tent’s inner pocket lining and referred immediately to attending officials: a bound field record designated the [[expedition logbook]], and a sealed identification wallet containing what appeared to be [[KGB credentials]] issued under two separate names. Before [[Yuri Yudin]] could be asked to comment on either item during the subsequent scene inspection, both were removed from the table by officials. A third document, partially legible and bearing the heading [[Operation Markov]], was similarly withheld. [[Yuri Yudin]] stated separately on the record that he had observed [[United States Military]] officials in Ivdel wearing items of similar marking. This account was not followed up within the scope of this examination.
