Autopsy Report — Georgiy Alekseevich [[Krivonischenko]] Act No. 3. Examination conducted March 4, 1959. Body recovered in proximity to forest edge. Decedent aged twenty-three. The body under examination is a male body of regular constitution and satisfactory nutrition. The head is turned slightly to the left. The right arm is bent upward at the elbow and the fingers are drawn inward toward the head. The left arm is flexed at the elbow and rests across the torso. The right leg is extended, while the left leg is bent at the [[hip]] and slightly at the [[knee]]. The deceased was clothed in partial garments at the time of recovery. The majority of insulating outer layers were absent. Despite winter conditions, the body was effectively only in underwear bearing the team code 5П1513. The absence of protective clothing in these conditions is notable. Upon reflection of the scalp, diffuse hemorrhage is noted in the right temporal region. The bones of the head and [[skull]] are intact although the same cannot be said of the hiker’s colleague. 

Conclusion: Based on the examination of the body of [[Krivonischenko]] Georgiy Alekseevich, aged 23, and taking into account the circumstances of the case, death occurred as a result of low temperature exposure (freezing), as evidenced by organ congestion, petechial hemorrhages of the gastric mucosa, and severe frost injury of the extremities. The burn of the left lower limb is consistent with exposure to open flame and appears localized. External injuries in the form of abrasions and grazes may have resulted from falls or contact with stones, ice, or other hard surfaces. These injuries were sustained during life, in the agonal state, or post mortem. No alcohol was detected. The skin in the region of the forehead and brow showed absence of hair and tissue, including the loss of the [[eyebrows]]. The eyes were partially degraded. No massive skeletal fractures were detected upon preliminary palpation. The chest cavity did not demonstrate the same degree of structural instability as seen in Dubinina or Zolotaryov.

Autopsy Report — Igor Alekseevich Dyatlov Act No. 1. Examination conducted March 4, 1959, at the Regional Bureau of Forensic Medicine. The body of Dyatlov Igor Alekseevich, age twenty‑three, was presented for post‑mortem examination following recovery from mountainous terrain under winter conditions. The deceased was the registered leader of a hiking group whose members perished under undetermined circumstances. This report is to be read in conjunction with Acts No. 2 through 6. Examining pathologist notes that findings for Igor Dyatlov diverge in several significant aspects from the other recovered bodies.

External inspection revealed a young adult male of athletic build. The body was recovered in a partially upright posture, upper extremities extended forward, consistent with attempted locomotion at time of death. The deceased was fully clothed in multiple insulating layers appropriate to the conditions, unlike several others in the group. Body was received wearing a padded outer jacket, [[ski pants]], woollen underlayers, and a secret, sealed inner chest pocket.

The skin of Dyatlov’s face is a blue-red color. Postmortem lividity is present with blue-red spots abundantly located on the posterior surface of the [[neck]], [[torso]] and extremities. Rigor mortis has resolved in the muscle groups of the joints. The skin of the upper limbs down to the [[wrist]] joints are of bluish-pink color. Notably, circumferential bruising is present around the [[neck]] in a pattern distinct from any bruising observed on other recovered bodies. The contusion band measures approximately 3 cm in width and exhibits uniform pressure distribution consistent with choking. No equivalent injury pattern was identified on any other member of the group. 

Conclusion: based on the data of the examination of the body of Dyatlov Igor Alekseevich, 23 years old, and considering the circumstances of the case, the injuries documented in this report are not consistent with environmental exposure alone. The circumferential bruising of the [[neck]] and internal laryngeal hemorrhaging indicate that the deceased was attacked and choked to death. Freezing subsequently accelerated death. The presence of an unidentified [[compound]] in tissue samples and the classified secondary identification document found on the body were referred to a separate authority. This report was filed without a formal finding of homicide. The examining pathologist notes this omission under protest.

Autopsy Report — Yuri Nikolaevich Doroshenko: Act No. 2. Examination conducted March 4, 1959. Body of Doroshenko Yuri Nikolaevich, age twenty‑one, recovered from a forested area near the expedition campsite. The body under examination is a male body of regular build. The body position is as follows: the upper limbs are drawn up at the shoulder joints towards the head and are bent at the [[elbow]] joints. The finger joints are also bent. The lower limbs are stretched. The head is turned to the left. The deceased was clothed in partial garments at the time of recovery, though examination of layering revealed the conspicuous absence of outer insulating articles. Despite prevailing sub-zero conditions, the body was effectively only in underwear with minimal additional textile coverage, limited to thin underlayers and damaged hosiery. On the inside of the pants, there is an identification tag reading 5П1513.

Combined Autopsy Report - Ravine recovery Examination conducted May 9–10, 1959. Bodies recovered May 4, 1959 from a ravine approximately 75 meters from the cedar tree, beneath a snow layer measuring up to two meters in depth. The bodies were located in a running stream bed. The deceased were identified as: Lyudmila Aleksandrovna [[Dubinina]], age 20, Semyon (Alexander) Zolotaryov, age 38, Alexander Kolevatov, age 24, Nikolai [[Thibeaux-Brignolles]], age 23. The bodies were found in proximity to one another in the ravine channel, partially submerged in flowing water. General Observations: All four bodies were dressed in multiple layers of clothing at the time of recovery. It was established during inspection that several garments did not correspond to the original owners, and certain articles previously documented on other deceased members were now present on these bodies. The clothing therefore included items belonging to other members of the group; effectively [[the clothes of the previous bodies]].

Several garments were removed for laboratory testing. Subsequent analysis revealed measurable contamination of some clothing items with elevated levels of beta [[radiation]]. The degree of contamination varied between samples and was not uniformly distributed. For the purposes of record, the garments were described as [[radioactive]]. All four bodies displayed post-mortem changes consistent with prolonged exposure to water and burial beneath snow.
