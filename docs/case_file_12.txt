Case File 12 — Ownership of Items Recovered at Scene
Ivdel. 5–7 March 1959.
Junior Counselor of Justice Ivanov, acting in accordance with Article 78 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, conducted a formal inspection of material evidence recovered from the vicinity of the tent site of the deceased tourist group, as well as from the forested area containing traces of a fire and the bodies later identified as [[Krivonischenko]] and Doroshenko. The inspection was carried out in the presence of representatives of the Ural Polytechnic Institute and members of the press. Among those present for purposes of identification was fourth-year student of UPI, [[Yuri Yudin]], who had separated from the expedition earlier due to illness and was the [[survivor]] therefore considered capable of recognizing group property based on personal familiarity, diary entries, and shared preparation of equipment.

The tent and surrounding equipment were found partially collapsed and covered with windblown snow. The tent was cut open [[from the inside]]. However, no conclusive evidence of structural compression by [[avalanche]] mass, [[snow slab]], or [[snow drift]] displacement was recorded at the time of inspection, although snow accumulation patterns were noted. All materials collected were tested for contamination of various kinds. Results of contamination tests are presented in a separate autopsy report.

1. A black backpack of worn construction  with a repaired side seam contained personal hygiene items, letters addressed to Kolmogorova, medical supplies including bandages and iodine, powdered milk tins, and a flashlight was assigned to [[Yuri Yudin]. According to Yuri, certain medical articles within the pack had been transferred to Kolmogorova at the Second Northern settlement when he departed the group. Garments included quilted outerwear, multiple pairs of woolen and vigogne socks, mended tent material and globes. When asked to confirm the route he had taken after departing the group, Yuri Yudin stated he had [[doubled back]] only as far as the Second Northern settlement to pass the medical equipment to Kolmogorova before continuing toward Vizhai by sled. No independent corroboration of this account was sought at this stage of proceedings.

2. Property Attributed to Igor Dyatlov Recovered articles included a fur vest, tarpaulin outer garments, woolen hat, coarse mittens, and ski equipment. Within the pockets were located a notebook, compass, pocketknife secured by cord, rope segments, and personal eyewear in a grey case. Of interest, a hidden pocket within Igor’s pack contained [[KGB credentials]] and a small gas mask. Yuri stated on the record that he was unaware of Igor’s relation to the intelligence organisation. A [[fur vest]] discovered on Dyatlov’s body was stated by [[Yuri Yudin]] to have originally belonged to him and to have been given to Igor on 28 January 1959, suggesting subsequent exchange among participants. Such exchanges complicate strict attribution of items. Additional red-stained [[fabric]], a torn [[strap]], and a broken [[frame]] possibly belonging to eyewear were documented but not conclusively linked to a particular owner. Results of contamination tests are presented in a separate autopsy report.

3. Property Attributed to Lyudmila Dubinina: a black backpack containing toiletries, broken spectacles, letters, elastic bandage, writing instruments, and a [[hiking badge]] pinned above the pocket was identified as Dubinina’s. Clothing layers included ski trousers, balaclava, felt boots, and multiple sock types. [[Yuri Yudin]] stated he had personally given her grey wool socks prior to his departure. The repetition of redistributed garments among members requires consideration in any analysis of final clothing configuration. Items located near the ravine area included additional torn [[textile]], fragments of [[insulation]], and distorted metal fittings. These materials were not immediately identifiable as belonging to any one member.

4. Property Attributed to Rustem Slobodin: a khaki backpack marked with a small rubber toy bear contained binoculars, camera equipment, repair tools, notebooks, and sewing materials. On the body were discovered multiple clothing layers including sweater, checkered shirt, insoles placed between garments, and four pairs of socks all for one foot  while the corresponding felt boot was absent. The asymmetry of footwear distribution remains unexplained in this protocol. Possible causes such as redistribution among participants, loss during movement, or removal under conditions of [[panic]] or [[disorientation]] cannot be assessed within this document. Detective Aleksandr identified separate items of interest not assigned to any particular individuals. These unassigned items are described in a separate forensic examination.

Supplementary Note — Added by Order of Investigating Prosecutor, June 1959: Subsequent review of the [[expedition logbook]] and the sealed credentials wallet, conducted after this examination was initially filed, established that both [[Igor Dyatlov]] and [[Yuri Yudin]] held active [[KGB credentials]] under the same issuing authority. Cross-referencing these documents with internal communications recovered separately confirmed that [[Operation Markov]] — an authorised field dispersal of experimental [[psychoactive agents]] targeting the northern Ural corridor — had been formally cancelled three days prior to the incident at Dyatlov’s insistence. Yudin, who is recorded as the operation’s co-designate, did not comply. Investigative reconstruction indicates that Yudin [[doubled back]] after his departure from the group on January 28th, reinstated the dispersal order through back channels, and re-entered the camp on the night of February 1st. The laryngeal injuries documented in the Dyatlov autopsy (compression fracturing inconsistent with environmental exposure) are consistent with the conclusion that Yudin [[attacked Dyatlov]] directly before the compounds took full effect on the remaining members. The group, already [[disoriented]] by the dispersal, fled the tent into sub-zero conditions without adequate clothing and did not survive. The case was closed without a finding of homicide. The logbook was not returned to this file.
