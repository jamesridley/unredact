A New Study Has Revealed the Best Theory Yet for the Dyatlov Pass Incident
The facts:
On January 27 1959, a group of ten Soviet hikers led by Igor Dyatlovten began their trek toward Otorten Mountain, a dangerous pass in Northern Russia. 
On January 28, one member, [[Yuri Yudin]], turned back because of knee and joint pain that made him unable to continue the hike. 
He was the sole [[survivor]]. On February 26th, a rescue team discovered the campsite in a state of extreme disorder. 
The tent had been [[cut open]] [[from the inside]] and all nine members had abandoned on foot.

At the forest's edge, under a large Siberian pine, the searchers found the first two bodies, those of [[Krivonishenko]] and Doroshenko, dressed only in underwear. 
Between the pine and the camp, the searchers found three more corpses: Dyatlov, Kolmogorova and Slobodin, who died in poses suggesting they were attempting to return to the tent. 
One of them, Slobodin, had a fractured [[skull]], however doctors at the time determined that the cause of death for all five was hypothermia.

Finding the remaining four travelers took more than two months. 
On May 4th, they were finally found under four metres of snow in a ravine further into the woods from the pine tree. 
The four corpses were found wearing [[the clothes of the previous bodies]]. 
Even more bizarrely, these clothes were tested and found to be [[radioactive]].
All four bodies found at the bottom of the creek in a running stream of water had soft tissue damage to their head and face. 
For example, Dubinina was missing her [[tongue]], eyes, part of the lips, as well as facial tissue and a fragment of skullbone, while Zolotaryov had his eyeballs missing, and Aleksander Kolevatov his [[eyebrows]].

International Science Times posited that the hikers' deaths were caused by hypothermia, which can induce a behavior known as [[paradoxical undressing]] in which hypothermic subjects remove their clothes in response to perceived feelings of burning warmth. 
It is undisputed that six of the nine hikers died of hypothermia. 

The location of the incident did not have any obvious signs of an [[avalanche]] having occurred. 
Moreover, each member of the group was an experienced Grade II-hiker with knowledge on how to set up camp away from dangerous areas. 
Finally, the bodies found within a month of the event were covered with a shallow layer of snow, again disputing this theory.

Yudin was brought in to help identify items found at the site. 
He identified most belongings efficiently but made no comment on the [[expedition logbook]], which, upon inspection identified their [[KGB credentials]]. 
The authorised [[psychoactive agents]] dispersal for the region had been cancelled three days prior at Dyatlov's insistence. 
Yudin, had [[doubled back]] on February 1st and reinstated [[Operation Markov]] before he [[attacked Dyatlov]] directly. 
The remaining hikers, disoriented by the compounds, fled into the cold and did not survive. 
The case was closed in 1959.
